                                  • On Violence Part 2

                                  Grant Oxford

                                   

                                  Frantz Fanon was a French psychoanalyst and philosopher. He was born in Martinique, a South American colony of France, and studied medicine and psychiatry in France. His anticolonial book, The Wretched of the Earth, featured a chapter named On Violence.

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                  Summary

                                   

                                  Two Main Arguments

                                   

                                  1. Violence, as committed by colonized subjects in the colonial context, is a unifying force.

                                  2. The duty of Europe is to rehabilitate man in the Third World, the duty of the Third World is to explain why, and the duty of the European proletariat is to wake up.

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                  Premises for Argument 1

                                  How is violence a unifying force?

                                   

                                   

                                  1.1 Atmospheric Violence

                                  But let us return to this atmospheric violence, this violence rippling under the skin. We have seen as it develops how a number of driving mechanisms pick it up and convey it to an outlet.

                                  (p. 31, para. 2)

                                   

                                  • Violence has the quality of spreading due to its atmosphere.

                                  • It occurs in one place, and is heard about across the globe.

                                  • It motivates the colonized subjects of a different region.

                                  • It cuts across the borders set by colonialism.

                                  Fanon gives the example of the Viatnamese Battle of Dien Bien Phu. In 1954, the Viatnamese military ended French colonial rule in the city. Consequently, colonized peoples across the globe asked: “How can we achieve our own Dien Bien Phu?”1

                                   

                                   

                                  1.2 Collective Identity

                                  Far from breaking the momentum, repression intensifies the progress made by by the national consciousness.

                                  (p. 32, para. 2)

                                   

                                  • Any group is bound to form a collective identity.

                                  • This is true in colonies.

                                  • Colonists try to stop a national identity from forming.

                                  • The bloodbath the colonists create only makes this identity stronger.

                                  • It reinforces the division between the oppressor and the oppressed.

                                  • Soon, every colonized subject has identified themself with the oppressed.

                                   

                                   

                                  1.3 Acceptance of Force

                                  …the colonized masses intuitively believe that their liberation must be achieved and can only be achieved by force.

                                  (p. 33, para. 4)

                                   

                                  • Colonists create an environment of force.

                                  • They enforce the colonial situation through force.

                                  • Thus, the colonized people need to use force to destroy the colonial situation.

                                  • But how can they hope to use force against a much stronger nation (technologically and economically)?

                                  The colonized peoples, these slaves of modern times, have run out of patience.

                                  (p. 34, para. 1)

                                   

                                  • For the colonized, force is the only option.

                                  • Despite the hopeless situation, they are united in their need to use force.

                                   

                                   

                                  1.4 Violence is Work

                                  For the colonized, this violence represents the absolute praxis. The militant therefore is one who works.

                                  (p. 44, para. 1)

                                   

                                  • Because violence is necessary in the consciousness of the colonized, it is seen as work.

                                  • In fact, it is their only work.

                                  • It is the only thing that can improve their status.

                                  • This work removes a person completely from the colonist’s side.

                                  • A violent enough act makes it impossible to return to the colonial system.

                                  • In establishes one’s place in the revolutionary community.

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                  Premises for Argument 2

                                  What are the duties of the Third World, Europe, and the European proletariat?

                                   

                                   

                                  2.1 What is Not Working

                                  When every three months we learn that the sixth or seventh U.S. Fleet is heading toward some coast or other,…the colonized or newly independent people get the impression they are being forced, whether they like it or not, into a frantic march.

                                  (p. 36, para. 2)

                                   

                                  • Both sides of the colonial conflict want to deescalate the violence, if possible.

                                  • The colonists try to do this by forcing decolonization.

                                  • They withdraw from their colonies as much as they can without hurting themselves.

                                  • But they cannot admit any fault.

                                  • They send journalists to prove that “everything went wrong as soon as we left.”

                                  Why does the First World want to force decolonization?

                                  • In the context of the cold war, it wants to reduce violence and the possibility of real war.

                                  • Global neutrality allows both sides to benefit from underdeveloped countries.

                                  • They brand their decolonization efforts as “helping the Third World.”

                                  • But if they really wanted to help, they would spend their money and engineers on raising global living standards rather than nuclear weaponry.

                                   

                                   

                                  2.2 The Duty of Europe is to Rehabilitate

                                  Europe is literally the creation of the Third World.

                                  (p. 58, para. 2)

                                   

                                  • European riches were harvested from the Third World.

                                  • Therefore, Europe owes the Third World its wealth.

                                  • Europe dehumanized the Third World to obtain this wealth.

                                  • Therefore, Europe owes the Third World rehabilitation.

                                   

                                   

                                  2.3 The Duty of the Third World is to Explain

                                  It is our [i.e., the Third World’s] duty, however, to tell and explain to the capitalist countries that they are wrong to think the fundamental issue of our time is the war between the socialist regime and them.

                                  (p. 61, para. 1)

                                   

                                  • The Third World is politically and economically unstable after the colonists leave.

                                  • The previous colonists are then scared to invest in the Third World.

                                  • But they need the Third World for its exports.

                                  • The Third World fuels capitalist industries.

                                  • Therefore, it is in the capitalists’ best interests to aid underdeveloped countries.

                                  • The Third World will also benefit from this.

                                  • Therefore, the Third World should explain this situation to foreigners.

                                   

                                  What [the Third World] does expect from those who have kept it in slavery for centuries it to help it rehabilitate man, and ensure his triumph everywhere, once and for all.

                                  (p. 61, para. 3)

                                   

                                  2.4 The Duty of the European Proletariat is to Wake Up

                                  This colossal task, which consists of reintroducing man into the world, man in his totality, will be achieved with the crucial help of the European masses who would do well to confess that they have often rallied behind the position of our common masters on colonial issues.

                                  (p. 62, para. 1)

                                   

                                  • Fanon is not very specific about this.

                                  • I don’t know if he wants only awareness, total action, or some middleground from the European proletariat.

                                  • Clearly, protest against the bourgeousie will be required.

                                  • The people need to redistribute wealth.

                                  • How can this be achieved?

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                   

                                  Commentary

                                   

                                  Marx’s Influence on Fanon

                                   

                                  • The influence is obvious, given Fanon’s frequent reference to the proletariat and the bourgeoisie.

                                  • Fanon’s model of colonialism, the compartmentalized model, is a form of classism.

                                  • The colonial struggle is a class struggle.

                                  • Fanon also wants the European proletariat to awaken and realize its own unity with the Third World. They both need to work together to overthrow their capitalist masters.

                                  • This is similar to Marx’s belief that once the proletariats of the world realize that they have a common enemy, they can finally unite and achieve freedom.

                                  • Fanon is in favor of socialism over capitalism, but he doesn’t think the Third World must resort to either.

                                  • Instead, they can make a new system as they establish themselves in the world.

                                   

                                   

                                  Hegel’s Influence

                                   

                                  Positive Liberty

                                  • In Reason in History, G.W.F. Hegel taught that historical progress is a teleology with the ultimate goal of universal freedom.

                                  • This is also the ultimate goal for Fanon.

                                  • Universal freedom includes positive liberty, meaning that people will not only be free externally (e.g. not in prison, not enslaved, etc.) butinternally free.

                                  • This means that people will be free from emotional or spiritual prison.

                                  • In the context of colonialism, Fanon sees violence as a way to reinvent oneself.

                                  • This is comparable to attaining positive liberty.

                                  • Through violence, the colonized subject regains his self-confidence.

                                  • When he finally decides to fight back, he frees himself from the chains of fatalism.

                                   

                                  The Cunning of History

                                  • Hegel coined the term, the “cunning of history,” which Fanon references on page 29:

                                  There is therefore a cunning of history which plays havoc with the colonies.

                                  (p. 29, para. 2)

                                  • For Hegel, this term meant the inevitability of the purpose of history.

                                  • He believed that universal freedom would be achieved, no matter how many people may fight against that ideal.

                                  • There was an inevitability to the purpose of history.

                                  • There is a similar inevitability in colonialism.

                                  • Everything the colonists do to protect colonialism leads to its demise.

                                  • They use force to divide the natives: the natives learn force as the primary method of change.

                                  • They use violence to attain their control: the natives respond with anticolonial violence.
